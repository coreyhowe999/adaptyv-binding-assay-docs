Construct & Tag Design

The standard tag construct we add to every binder for expression, purification, and immobilization — what each part does, where it sits, and why it stays clear of the binding site.

Reference Applies to Binding & Characterization ~4 min read

You submit your binder as a bare sequence — no tags needed. Before expression, we append a standard tag construct that handles three jobs at once: expression quantification, purification, and immobilization on the assay instrument. This page shows exactly what we add, where it goes, and how we keep it away from the binding interface.

i
Nothing to do on your side. If you submitted sequences without tags, that's expected — we add them for you. The only optional input is tag placement (C- vs N-terminus); see Placement below.

The Twin-Strep-tag does double duty: it's how we purify the protein and how we capture it onto the sensor. On our platform, capture is the purification step.

Why these tags

Twin-Strep-tag — a high-affinity, well-behaved handle for Strep-Tactin XT. We use it both to purify the binder and to immobilize it on the SPR sensor, so a single tag covers the whole workflow.

split-GFP11 — a small fragment that reconstitutes fluorescence with a complementary GFP1–10 fragment, letting us quantify expression without a separate readout. See Split-GFP quantification for the method.

We do not cleave purification tags. Instead we choose placement (or follow your preference) so the tag stays clear of the binding site — which is what the structures below are for.

Tag placement

C-terminal is the default — in our hands it gives the most consistent expression across protein types. You can request N-terminal placement instead if the C-terminus is functionally important for your design; just flag it at submission.

The construct is designed so the tag sits away from the binding interface regardless of scaffold. The predicted structures below show the tag position relative to the paratope across representative protein types.

How the tag is used in the assay

On the SPR instrument (Carterra LSA), the Twin-Strep-tag is the capture handle:

  1. Surface prep. A carboxymethyl-dextran sensor is activated by amine coupling, and Strep-Tactin XT is covalently coupled to the surface.
  2. Capture. Your Twin-Strep-tagged binder is captured onto the Strep-Tactin XT surface as the immobilized ligand. This capture step also serves as the purification — no separate purification is required on our platform.
  3. Kinetics. The target is flowed over the captured binder as the analyte in a concentration series (single-cycle kinetics). Targets are delivered in solution and are not biotinylated.
✓
Orientation summary: binder = captured on the chip via its Twin-Strep-tag; target = flowed in solution. Capturing via the tag also keeps the binder uniformly oriented, which improves data quality versus random amine coupling of the binder itself.

What you need to do

Nothing — we add the construct automatically. Optionally, at submission you may request N-terminal placement instead of the default C-terminal, or flag any design where the terminus is functionally critical so we can confirm placement.
